How Trump’s Covid Relief Agricultural Subsidies Benefited the Biggest Farms

By H CONLEY

Agricultural Subsidies have never been an equal opportunity game. For generations the biggest farms have received a disproportionate level of assistance, but under President Trump this chasm grew wider.

Trump introduced two Ad-hoc programs to help farmers deal with the ramifications of his trade war with China and the COVIDD-19 pandemic. The Coronavirus Food Assistance Program (CFAP 1) favored larger, industrial scale grain and meat producers over small scale, polyculture farms. The Biden administration continued these payments through CFAP 2 in 2021 and has dedicated $5,064,904,910 to corn production alone. Much of this was to mitigate the losses due to decreased need for biofuel.

This disparity is clear when comparing payments from these programs for Iowa and New York. New York State is dominated by small farms making less than $50,000 a year according to USDA data. Iowa is dominated by industrial scale, mono-cropped grain operations.

This favoring of industrial agriculture encourages the environmental degradation caused by monocropping cereal grains; erosion, run off of fertilizers, pesticides and herbicides into waterways, and desertification.

According to Environmental Working Group (EWG), the Biden administration can remedy this disparity by ending the program or adjusting it to target small farms; increasing funding for conservation programs and incentivizing sustainable production practices; and “Changing farm subsidy programs to end USDA’s racist legacy. Stricter payment and income limits that would send payments to small farms, instead of the largest farms, would benefit Black, Latino and Asian American farmers, who often own smaller farms than white farmers do.”
